*Contributed by William*

Many people, especially younger students who just joined, ask me when they should start preparing for college applications. I feel there shouldn’t be a single fixed point in time. Looking back now, the college application process felt more like exploratory work done during FP and DP1 — for example, taking part in 知行, joining project week, or even lighter, smaller things like chatting with friends, going out, or simply going to classes. Through that process you might discover your passion, or you might not have a very clear goal but develop some rough ideas. Then, when you start preparing application materials in the summer before DP2 or in the first half of the DP2 term, it becomes a retrospective, a connecting the dots process; it’s very spontaneous and doesn’t require being overly deliberate. Some people will think this is a romanticized take and not very realistic. But even from a pragmatic, utilitarian perspective I think the answer is the same. Take CS in STEM for example: if someone doesn’t even know the basic areas in CS (like AI, systems, CS theory, etc.) and what each area does, how could they write an essay that moves an admissions officer or find a school that suits them? I’m not saying you must have a deep understanding of every field in the major while still in high school. If you’re truly interested in a direction, you’ll be thinking about it constantly — eating, sleeping, you can’t stop thinking about it — that’s probably what people mean by passion. But there’s no need to be anxious about this, and it certainly doesn’t mean you must have all these areas figured out in DP1 or FP. In the past few years I’ve increasingly believed: never wait until the timing is perfect (i.e., everything is ready) to start something. As the saying goes, "sharpening your knife won’t delay the woodcutting" — many times you only realize your passion after you’ve already joined a 知行 program or started a project. Those are the exploratory activities I mentioned earlier.

So what if by DP2 you still find nothing particularly interesting? Then maybe no one can save you — just kidding. Actually, at that point you can look for subjects you mildly like, even if it’s not a passion, or subjects that come naturally to you; that’s more than enough. And if you feel equally indifferent to every subject, and you’re applying to American colleges, congratulations — most U.S. schools offer an "undecided major" option, so you can enter and explore general coursework before declaring a major.

Next you should start choosing schools. But before that, I want to introduce the university counselling office. I remember there were about five counsellors at my school, and each of us was assigned one in the second semester of DP1. Each counsellor’s role is to help us apply successfully to the colleges we want to attend — in other words, they’re the primary contact for everything related to applications. To be specific, I’ll use my college counselor Alison as an example. I’ve always thought Alison was an incredibly, incredibly good counsellor. To be honest, I was somewhat socially anxious at the time — even talking with strangers made me nervous — so I was very anxious before my first meeting with Alison; to me it felt like a "life-defining" moment. But the instant I opened the door and saw her, I felt an immediate sense of relief. All my nervousness disappeared in a blink. After that we met periodically, and once DP2 began the meetings became a bit more frequent. I think the most important thing between you and your counselor is to build a rapport; you can’t manufacture this by force (and trying to force it usually fails). You need to fully trust them and open up — tell them all your thoughts, questions, and confusions — so they can understand you better and help you in a targeted way. Again, it’s a spontaneous process. Here’s a little secret: the counselor’s recommendation is the most important of the three required recommendations, bar none. Because the counselor talks with you about many philosophical things, compared with the other two teacher recommendations they understand what kind of person you are, how you think about high-level issues, and your maturity — exactly the qualities U.S. colleges value most.

Alright, now we should get to school selection. This Survival Guide was originally meant to be a "get-you-started" companion, and since I’ve introduced the counselling office, this guide’s mission could be considered complete — especially given how specialized school selection is. Still, I want to share some perspectives from a UWC graduate about things I wish I’d known back then.
