Drinking two to three cups of coffee daily may reduce dementia risk
PAM BELLUCK
February 11, 2026
Researchers said that after drinking more than two and a half cups of coffee daily, the benefits plateaued, possibly because there's a limit to the body's ability to metabolize caffeine.

If you've always felt that a few cups of espresso or Earl Grey tea daily make your mind sharper, new scientific research suggests you might be right.
A new large-scale study confirms that coffee and tea do offer cognitive benefits — provided the beverages contain caffeine, and are consumed in moderation: two to three cups of coffee or one to two cups of tea daily.
Researchers tracked 131,821 participants for 43 years, and the study reported that people who maintained this intake for decades had a significantly lower probability of developing dementia compared to those who rarely or never consumed caffeine.
“This rigorous, large-scale, long-term study, covering both men and women, shows an association between drinking two to three cups of coffee daily and a reduced risk of dementia,” said Aladin Shadyab, an associate professor of public health and medicine at the University of California San Diego, who was not involved in the study.

The study, published Monday in The Journal of the American Medical Association (JAMA), did not prove that caffeine directly causes these benefits; it’s possible other compounds in caffeinated beverages protect drinkers' brain health. However, independent experts noted that the study had adjusted for numerous other factors, including health status, medication use, diet, education level, socioeconomic status, family history of dementia, body mass index, smoking status, and psychiatric conditions.
The association between caffeine and reduced dementia risk held true regardless of whether participants carried genetic risk factors for Alzheimer's disease or other types of dementia. The study, funded by the National Institutes of Health (NIH), did not differentiate between specific types of dementia.
Experts stated that some previous studies had not found cognitive benefits from caffeine, but these studies often had limitations, such as shorter follow-up periods or only a one-time assessment of diet. Shadyab said this new research aligns with a growing body of evidence, “all suggesting that caffeinated coffee may reduce the risk of age-related chronic diseases.”
Researchers tracked two long-running studies of healthcare professionals: female participants in the Nurses' Health Study and male participants in the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study. At the start of the study, most participants were around 45 or in their early 50s, and they completed multiple questionnaires related to diet, health, and lifestyle. During the study period, 11,033 participants were diagnosed with dementia, based on death certificates or physician diagnoses.
Compared to those who consumed almost no caffeine, people who drank one to five cups (about 220 milliliters per cup) of caffeinated coffee daily had an approximately 20% lower risk of dementia; those who drank at least one cup of caffeinated tea daily had an approximately 15% lower risk.
However, Daniel Wang, a senior author of the study and an epidemiologist specializing in neurodegenerative diseases at Mass General Brigham, said that after drinking more than 2.5 cups of coffee daily, the benefits no longer increased, possibly because the human body cannot metabolize any more bioactive compounds from coffee and tea.

Daniel Wang, who drinks about three cups of coffee and three cups of green tea himself daily, stated that the study found no negative effects from high caffeine intake. However, Fangfang Zhang, an epidemiologist at the Friedman School of Nutrition Science and Policy at Tufts University, who was not involved in this new study, noted that some research indicates excessive caffeine intake can disrupt sleep and exacerbate anxiety, thereby harming health.
Zhang stated that in a 2025 study on caffeine intake and reduced mortality, her team found that “more than three cups daily did not provide additional benefits.” She also noted that adding too much milk or sugar can counteract caffeine's benefits for reducing mortality, and this dementia-related study did not track the addition of milk and sugar.
In addition to dementia, this new study also collected assessment data on subjective cognitive decline — that is, people's perception of their own declining memory and thinking abilities, which is often an early sign of dementia. Participants with higher caffeine intake reported a lower probability of subjective cognitive decline.
Approximately 17,000 female participants (all over 70 years old) also regularly completed cognitive tests. Daniel Wang stated that those with higher caffeine intake performed slightly better on tests within their age group, and the rate of cognitive decline was slowed by about seven months.
Scientists believe that caffeine protects brain health because its compounds can reduce neuroinflammation and improve vascular function. Some studies also suggest that caffeine can enhance insulin sensitivity, thereby preventing diabetes, which is a risk factor for dementia.
For those who don't drink caffeinated beverages, Shadyab said the study's findings “don't necessarily mean people should be encouraged to drink coffee, but for those who currently have a coffee-drinking habit, these results can reassure them — drinking coffee may reduce the risk of dementia.”

Zhang suggested that those who don't consume caffeine could “give it a try,” recommending starting with small doses in case of caffeine sensitivity.
The association between caffeine and reduced dementia risk was most significant among people under 75. Daniel Wang stated that cognitive impairment develops over decades, “so, if healthy habits can be changed earlier, before middle age, the benefits will be greater.”
Since the study participants were all healthcare professionals, this group might not be representative of the general population. However, Zhang pointed out that those with higher caffeine intake were more likely to smoke and drink alcohol, suggesting their health status might not necessarily be superior to that of average Americans.
Researchers couldn't rule out all factors that might influence the results. They wrote, for instance, whether some individuals chose to drink decaffeinated coffee because they already had health issues that increase dementia risk, “rather than decaf coffee directly leading to an increased risk”?
They also couldn't determine which beverage is better for the brain: Darjeeling tea or matcha? Sumatran coffee or Colombian coffee? Is it a six-dollar Americano, or the bland, free coffee from the office machine?
