*Contributed by William*

This piece uses the topic of school selection to talk about what I only learned about American universities after starting college. Maybe I was just ignorant before—I found that universities here are very different from what I had imagined.

First, I used to think private universities were arbitrarily better than public ones except for higher tuition. But that's not really the case. To understand why, we should start from first principles: follow the money.

Many people, myself included, assume private universities are wealthy because tuition is high, so they must have abundant resources. But for a large school, even high tuition from each student is only a drop in the bucket.

Where do schools' funds come from? A large portion actually comes from the federal government. People encountering this for the first time may find it counterintuitive—I'll admit I assumed schools were either private or state-run, and except for service academies the federal government wouldn't be that involved. In fact, agencies like the NSF, DOE, NIH, and the Department of Defense/Armed Forces fund most research spending, especially in STEM. Other agencies also run funding projects. So in US universities a STEM professor's main job is often not just doing research or lecturing, but fundraising. Laboratories often resemble startups: the professor is like the founder/CEO who sets direction and goes out to raise money. How do they raise money? By writing proposals—'writing a grant'—submitting them like bids when the federal agency posts a project, and then waiting. At first glance it may seem only professors and federal agencies are involved. Where does the university fit in? When a proposal is awarded and funds are about to arrive, the university steps in to collect 'toll'—the overhead. For example: a professor wins a $1,000,000 DOE grant; the DOE pays that $1,000,000 to the professor but also pays the university an additional amount based on the Overhead Rate. Federal overhead rates can be high—even up to 80%—which means that a $1,000,000 grant costs the agency $1.8M in total, or in other words $800k does not go directly to the professor's lab. Whether this is reasonable is not my point here: schools provide large administrative and facilities support without which professors and students couldn't focus on research, but those support systems can also be bloated. The takeaway is that much of a university's money actually comes from the federal government; the university provides a platform, but how 'well-off' a lab is often depends on the professor's academic brand, much like multi-strategy hedge funds. This helps explain why American academia is pluralistic instead of monopolized by a few elite institutions, though there's more to say later.

I can already hear someone asking: what about endowment funds? Private universities have wealthy alumni who donate a lot—does that not outweigh federal funds? There's truth to that, but there are practical issues. The main one is flexibility in spending. Endowments are essentially family/trust-like funds: you mostly can spend the investment returns, not the principal. Typically only about 5–10% of the endowment is available each year (I haven't done rigorous research, but that figure is similar to good hedge funds). Even if a school gets returns, donors usually restrict the use. You often see named scholarships or buildings—that means the donor designated the funds for a specific purpose, not for general use.

This chart roughly reflects university endowments; the blue-highlighted ones (for example the University of Texas system and Texas A&M) are public universities—Texas schools really have money. But public universities have many students, so they face a 'large total but small per-capita' problem, reminiscent of Wen Jiabao's famous remark: any small problem multiplied by 1.3 billion becomes huge, and any large total divided by 1.3 billion shrinks. For a public university, no matter how large the endowment or federal grants, per-student resources can look small when spread across so many students. But is that the whole story?

Let's go back to the source: why do state universities enroll so many students? The reason is simple: state governments provide substantial funding each year, and laws often require that a high proportion of admitted students be in-state (commonly 80%+). That makes it relatively easy for in-state high schoolers to get into state schools, but it also means the incoming student body is uneven. For our UWC students, what does this mean? If your ability is fixed, you might be in the top 50% at an Ivy League school but the top 10% or even top 5% at many state schools. Be prepared that the top 5% at a state school are often on par with the top 5% at an Ivy. The very best students are comparable; state schools just have a longer tail. Note also that large state universities don't necessarily lack resources compared to private schools—on strong departments they may far exceed most private universities (for example, UC Berkeley and UIUC have total CS resources that beat almost all private schools except the very top ones). So for students who are not already at the absolute top level (I mean IOI-level international-competition medalists), you might actually get more resources at a strong state school. CMU once had a professor hiring RAs who explicitly required candidates to have IOI/IMO scores above a certain threshold (which changed yearly)—that illustrates the phenomenon.

So I think choosing a school should be about finding a place that truly fits you, not being carried away by reputation. To be honest, after coming to the US I realized undergraduate education at some schools is like luxury goods, especially for STEM: if you're a flashy, high-profile person, these schools will suit you—you'll more easily find your place there (not necessarily greater achievements, but a vibe-wise resonance that can make you happier). If you're low-key and pragmatic, a Hermès bag is often less useful than a canvas tote. This is what I most want to tell younger students: if someone had told me this when I was applying, I wouldn't have chased fame and applied to schools that now seem a poor fit; I might have chosen a better match and left spots for others to go to the top schools.

Finally, why is American academia so diverse instead of dominated by a few institutions? Federal funding is only an indirect cause. One direct reason is how brutally hard it is to get a faculty position now. Basically only the very best PhDs from top programs can find decent jobs at top schools. In CS, it's not an exaggeration that almost no one gets a tenure-track assistant professor (TTAP) job without doing a postdoc; many IOI medalists did two, three, or even more years of postdoc before landing a position. Top universities compete fiercely to hire faculty. For example, UIUC's CS department recently hired AI-area TTAPs who, prior to joining, already had tens of thousands of citations. AI tends to have higher citation counts than many other CS areas and other disciplines, but those numbers are still staggering. Hiring is much more random than undergraduate admissions: to hire a professor you need no one on the search committee to dislike them—and ideally at least one person to champion them. That leads many outstanding PhDs to end up at less elite schools. For students, this means you don't have to go to the very top school to meet excellent professors. Do top schools have advantages? Yes. Using Jin Yong's wuxia novels as an analogy, the top five or ten schools may have several master-level 'Eastern Heretic'/'Western Poison' professors, and a batch of 'Guo Jing'-level professors. More ordinary schools will still have a few 'Guo Jing'-level professors, so the gap isn't as large as people imagine.

For students not planning to do research, having many high-quality professors matters a lot: it means courses will be comprehensive and you can find a faculty member in whatever field you want to study. This is a weakness of small liberal arts colleges: despite their small size and emphasis on teaching quality, each department tends to be small, so they can only offer courses in mainstream directions. Schools like Barnard or Wellesley mitigate this by cross-registration with nearby top universities—Wellesley students can take classes at Harvard and MIT, which fills resource gaps.

In the end, a school mostly provides opportunities, resources, and a platform—your growth still depends on you. As Chairman Wang Jia Peng often tells us: 'Each of you is the chairperson of your own life's company; we (teachers, parents, the school) are only directors.' So wherever you end up, what's more important is gradually clarifying what kind of person you want to be and what life you want, and then walking steadily toward it. Many things won't have immediate answers, but with effort and persistence you'll gradually find your path. Often, more important than external labels is whether you're happy living your life (see [About Setbacks]({{ '/guides/default/about-setbacks/' | relative_url }}) for more). If the competitive framing above feels overwhelming, or you simply love a school even if it's not the objectively 'best' match, that's perfectly fine. Choosing a school isn't a multiple-choice test with one right answer—it's picking a place suitable for living and growth; the only essential question is whether you can be comfortable and happy there.

That's the academic side of American schools pretty much covered. But college life is not only academics—there are many other equally or more important factors. Chief among them is location. Sometimes I think location matters even more than academics because it directly affects travel convenience—for leisure, returning home, job onsite interviews, or conference travel. I once went to New York for an event: even students not studying in New York, like those from Princeton in nearby New Jersey, can get there in an hour by taxi. But for someone like me studying in rural central Illinois, reaching New York meant taking a bus (over three hours) or two hours by taxi to Chicago, then flying an hour—assuming no delays, it still takes half a day, which is inconvenient. For interviews, travel fatigue affects performance; you usually have to arrive the day before, meaning you need to stay two nights. So I think having access to a major airport is a practical criterion. Places like the Duke/UNC/NC State area are great: the three schools are roughly fifteen minutes' drive from each other and twenty minutes to an airport, without the hustle of a big city.
