In the so-called elite narratives circulating on social media, nothing is more elegant than the image of coding with one hand and reading Shakespeare with the other. Picture it: late at night in a café, CUDA kernels pulsing across the screen on the left, an open copy of *Hamlet* resting just so on the right. It happens to be exactly the recipe that certain elite-education pipelines hold most dear. This "polymath" persona is incredibly seductive because it precisely taps into an ancient craving: to announce, through a self-narrative that is sufficiently romantic and sufficiently deep, that you belong to a particular kind of person. The Shakespeare-and-code combo just happens to supply that narrative, hinting that you possess both the hardness of logic and the thickness of a soul. Change the era, change the circle — the symbols will change, but the craving won't. The logic of this class signaling is typically built in two steps. First, technical depth establishes that you are not at the same intellectual level as ordinary people — after all, those who can truly master such things are few. Second, a layer of romance and depth gets draped over that foundation, announcing that you are not just a clever executor but a person with a soul. Of course, some skip step one entirely and begin right at step two. For them the technical work doesn't matter; what matters is that innate spiritual temperament. Ordinary programmers, meanwhile, are the implicit control group in this narrative: they understand the technical but stop there, so they remain mere tools. Only the elite who possess both intellect and soul are truly scarce.

*Note: this piece isn't about people who genuinely love both — it's about those who use the liberal-arts pose as class signaling.*

Why are so many people obsessed with Shakespeare? I don't think it's because those words actually strike them — it's because the image of "a person who reads Shakespeare" carries an indefinable, aristocratic aura that hovers above the merely practical. (The symbol can of course be anything — appreciating literary works, studying quantum mechanics, working through abstract algebra, or even switching to wine tasting, classical music, or meditation — as long as it's impractical and has a high enough barrier to entry.) But this kind of consumption of the liberal-arts sensibility is precisely the greatest misunderstanding of what it means to be cultured. The real Shakespeare is anything but gentle — he lays bare the bloody workings of power and the darkest corners of human nature, and those who actually read him tend not to become more romantic, but more unsettled. Here, the liberal-arts sensibility becomes a shared marionette for two camps: some technical people use it to dress up the monotony of a technical identity, while some artsy people use it to plaster a facade over an intellectual void. Together they build an absurd consensus — that a liberal-arts sensibility is a "vibe" that can exist independently of rigorous logic, a posture to be struck rather than a field to actually enter. It's like the many people who love talking about sustainable development — they can recite carbon-neutrality slogans by heart, yet can't even explain how the most basic energy system actually works. The symbol gets consumed, but the substance behind it is never touched.

The most intoxicating delusion of class signaling goes like this: when someone dresses themselves up with labels like "deep" or "romantic," they don't think they're performing — they genuinely believe they simply are that kind of person, one who has both technical chops and a soul. The most insidious toxicity of this class signaling is that it instrumentalizes the liberal-arts sensibility — dulling the rigor of the technical while hollowing out the depth of the humanistic. The way out of this performance isn't to become even more "deep"; it's to stop performing at all. And there's a concept that's easily confused here: mediocrity. Mediocrity and not-performing look alike, but they're actually quite different. Someone can quietly go deep into one thing without needing an audience; someone can also drift through four years — or even a lifetime — treating code or writing as nothing more than a way to earn a living. The former is non-performative depth; the latter is non-performative ordinariness. What they share is that each is a choice made where no camera is watching. If you'd rather live a plainer life, calmly admitting that you neither read the logic of code nor sit through a Shakespearean sonnet is already a complete form of honesty — it needs no comparison and no higher vantage point to justify itself.

The core of class signaling, I think, is mistaking the fantasy of intellectual depth for depth itself. And the reason this substitution can work is that it almost always happens from outside the field. People outside can't see the fine-grained texture inside a field, only a silhouette — and from that silhouette an imagination about "the kind of person I am" is born. This problem isn't unique to those who style themselves as cultured; academia has its own isomorphic version. I once came across a biting critique from a professor at Texas A&M:

> Some people look down on researchers who churn out one small paper after another, spending years thinking they're brewing a breakthrough on the level of [Kaiming He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiming_He) or [Fei-Fei Li](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fei-Fei_Li). Then, when they finally produce something, they realize it's not even as good as what big labs casually churn out on the side.

In reality, those who look down on this kind of prolific, incremental publishing and those who pride themselves on being cultured share the same superiority. On the surface they're doing utterly different things: one is talking about the quality of output, the other about identity and temperament. But at a deeper level, both are using an imagined "the kind of person I am" to stand in for real output. Those holding out for the grand hit use the self-image of "I'm working on something big" to dodge the anxiety of having nothing to show for their years. Those who style themselves as cultured use the self-image of "I have soul" to dodge the fact that they haven't actually dug deep into any field. Both are treating narrative as result, and posture as action.

I have always believed that deciphering the ancient pronunciation of a Chinese character is just as important as discovering a minor planet. Dignity in any field is born only of the down-to-earth labor pains of solving complex problems. A lot of these down-to-earth moments don't photograph well, and wouldn't look right staged in a café: someone gets pulled in by a specific, stubborn problem and decides not to let go. That refusal to let go has dignity, and that dignity is the same across every field. The people who rank disciplines into high and low are never the ones actually doing the work — they're the ones watching from the sidelines. What I'd rather see is someone weighing the tension in a word the way they wring every drop of performance out of code — always with reverence for underlying logic, chasing that pure excitement that refuses to be defined by disciplinary boundaries. And if someone finds themselves drawn to neither? That's okay too, because for a lot of people our age, calmly accepting your own ordinariness often requires even greater courage. After all, true elegance has never been about living inside some carefully constructed persona — it is the willingness, always, to embrace that imperfect but real self.

## Appendix: A Short Honesty Checklist

In a place like UWC, where expectations run sky-high, it's hard not to get swept up in some perfect-elite narrative. If you have ever wondered whether you yourself might be doing a little Class Signaling, try sitting alone late one night and gently asking yourself a few questions:

**On the audience:**
If no one in your life ever knew what you were reading or studying — if no one gave you any credit for your liberal-arts polish — would the words or the logic still move you on their own? If that pure joy is gone, it may be because you have been carrying a script written for someone else's eyes.

**On superiority:**
Have you, somewhere beneath the surface, been using this cultured identity as a defense mechanism? When you hit a wall in one domain, do you soothe the setback by telling yourself "at least I have depth elsewhere"? Real depth is not a step stool for looking down on others; it is the ground beneath your feet when you walk through a valley.

**On sanctuary:**
Are you consuming a symbol, or experiencing the spirit behind it? Whether you retreat into books or retreat into code, admitting that you are tired and need a space to breathe should be encouraged. What we need to distinguish is this: are you seeking a sanctuary that lets you keep living, or building a moat that advertises how different you are?

**On awkward honesty:**
Do you allow yourself to be inelegant? Real exploration of any field is almost always clumsy in its early stages. If you cannot bring yourself to admit that you are, in some field, really just a fumbling beginner — because it would damage some persona you've built — then the persona itself has become the shackle on your growth.

**On hierarchy:**
When you talk about the things you actually do, is there an invisible disciplinary map in your head — one where certain fields are worth mentioning and others are beneath notice? If so, ask yourself: was that map drawn after you'd actually gone deep into some field, or was it a pre-set assumption from before you ever stepped onto the court? The first is earned experience; the second is just a shortcut you've quietly granted yourself.

**A final word:**

There is no shame in needing a persona from time to time; it can even serve as a social lubricant. But stay vigilant — do not let those signals become the wall between you and the real world, or between you and your real self. In an age where everyone wants to be the protagonist of some perfect-elite narrative, what I would rather pay tribute to is the courage of those who can admit they did not really understand Shakespeare, and the honesty of those who can calmly accept that their code is, in the end, nothing special. Because we never need to reach for another kind of altitude to cover up our truest selves.
