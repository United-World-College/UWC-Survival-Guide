Drinking two to three cups of coffee daily may lower dementia risk
PAM BELLUCK
Feb. 11, 2026
Researchers say that after two and a half cups of coffee daily, the benefits plateau, possibly because the human body has a limit to its ability to metabolize caffeine.

If you feel that a few cups of espresso or Earl Grey tea daily make your mind sharper, new scientific research suggests you might be right.
A new large-scale study confirms that coffee and tea do offer cognitive benefits — provided the drinks contain caffeine and are consumed in moderation: two to three cups of coffee or one to two cups of tea daily.
Researchers tracked 131,821 participants for 43 years, and the study reported that people who maintained this consumption level for decades had a significantly lower probability of developing dementia than those who consumed little or no caffeine.
“This rigorous, large-scale, long-term study covering both men and women shows that drinking two to three cups of coffee daily is associated with a reduced risk of dementia,” said Aladdin Shadyab, an associate professor of public health and medicine at the University of California San Diego (who was not involved in the study).

The study, published Monday in JAMA, did not prove that caffeine directly causes these benefits; it's also possible that other compounds in caffeinated beverages protect drinkers' brain health. However, independent experts noted that the study had adjusted for numerous other factors, including health conditions, medication use, diet, education level, socioeconomic status, family history of dementia, BMI, smoking status, and mental illness.
The association between caffeine and reduced dementia risk held true regardless of whether participants carried genetic risk factors for Alzheimer's disease or other types of dementia. This study, funded by the National Institutes of Health, did not differentiate between specific types of dementia.
Experts stated that some previous studies had not found cognitive benefits from caffeine, but these studies often had limitations, such as shorter follow-up periods or only a one-time dietary assessment. Shadyab noted that this new study is consistent with a growing body of research, “all indicating that caffeinated coffee may reduce the risk of age-related chronic diseases.”
Researchers tracked two long-running studies of health professionals: female participants in the Nurses' Health Study and male participants in the Health Professionals Follow-Up Study. At the start of the study, most participants were around 45 or in their early 50s, and they completed multiple questionnaires related to diet, health, and lifestyle. During the study, a total of 11,033 participants were diagnosed with dementia, based on death certificates or physician diagnoses.
Compared to those who consumed almost no caffeine, people who drank one to five cups (about 220 milliliters per cup) of caffeinated coffee daily had about a 20% lower risk of dementia; those who drank at least one cup of caffeinated tea daily had about a 15% lower risk.
However, Daniel Wang, a senior author of the study and an epidemiologist specializing in neurodegenerative diseases at Mass General Brigham, stated that after drinking more than 2.5 cups of coffee daily, the benefits no longer increased, possibly because the human body cannot metabolize more of the bioactive compounds in coffee and tea.

Daniel Wang, who himself drinks about three cups of coffee and three cups of green tea daily, said the study found no negative effects from high caffeine intake. However, Fangfang Zhang, an epidemiologist at Tufts University's Friedman School of Nutrition Science and Policy, who was not involved in this new study, noted that some research indicates excessive caffeine intake can disrupt sleep and exacerbate anxiety, thereby harming health.
Zhang Fangfang stated that in a 2025 study on caffeine intake and reduced mortality, her team found that “more than three cups daily did not provide additional benefits.” She also noted that adding too much milk or sugar can counteract caffeine's benefits for reducing mortality, and this dementia-related study did not track the addition of milk and sugar.
Besides dementia, this new study also collected data on subjective cognitive decline — that is, people's perception of a decline in their own memory and thinking abilities, which is often an early sign of dementia. Participants with higher caffeine intake reported a lower probability of subjective cognitive decline.
Approximately 17,000 female participants (all over 70) also regularly completed cognitive tests. Daniel Wang stated that those with higher caffeine intake performed slightly better on tests compared to others in their age group, and the rate of cognitive decline was delayed by about seven months.
Scientists believe that caffeine protects brain health because its components can reduce neuroinflammation and improve vascular function. Some studies also show that caffeine can enhance insulin sensitivity, thereby preventing diabetes, which is a risk factor for dementia.
For those who do not consume caffeinated beverages, Shadyab said the study findings “do not necessarily mean people should be encouraged to drink coffee, but for those who currently have a coffee habit, these results can reassure them — coffee might reduce dementia risk.”

Zhang Fangfang suggested that those who don't consume caffeine could “give it a try,” recommending starting with small doses in case of caffeine sensitivity.
The association between caffeine and reduced dementia risk was most significant among people under 75. Daniel Wang stated that cognitive impairment develops over decades, “so if healthy habits can be changed earlier, before middle age, the benefits will be greater.”
Because the study participants were all health professionals, this group may not be representative of the general population. However, Zhang Fangfang pointed out that those with higher caffeine intake were more likely to smoke and drink alcohol, suggesting their health status might not necessarily be superior to that of average Americans.
Researchers could not rule out all factors that might influence the results. For example, they wrote, did some people choose to drink decaffeinated coffee because they already had health issues that increased dementia risk, “rather than decaffeinated coffee directly leading to increased risk”？
Nor could they determine which drink is better for the brain: Darjeeling tea or matcha? Sumatran coffee or Colombian coffee? Is it a six-dollar Americano, or the bland free coffee from the office machine?
