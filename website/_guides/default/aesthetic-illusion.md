In the so-called elite narratives circulating on social media, nothing is more elegant than the image of coding with one hand and reading Shakespeare with the other. Picture it: late at night in a café, CUDA kernels pulsing across the screen on the left, an open copy of *Hamlet* resting just so on the right. It happens to be exactly the recipe that some certain elite-education pipelines hold most dear. This "polymath" persona is incredibly seductive, as it perfectly taps into the yearning for class aesthetics and romantic narratives. But if you spend enough time in a major research lab, you'll realize that this narrative is often a highly dangerous form of aesthetic compensation.

*Note: this piece isn't about people who genuinely love both — it's about those who use the liberal-arts pose as class signaling.*

Why are so many people obsessed with Shakespeare? Because when someone feels their brain suffocating while wrestling with system deadlocks or optimizing CUDA kernels, treating humanistic depth as a comfort zone becomes the most dignified escape. But this is itself an illusion — the real Shakespeare is anything but gentle. He lays bare the bloody workings of power and the darkest corners of human nature, and unpacking him is no easier than unpacking any complex distributed system. What people call "retreating into the humanities" is really just retreating into a fantasy of the humanities. The same goes the other way: the "code machines" who stack logic relentlessly yet refuse to reflect on the meaning of their work are practicing another form of the very same escape.

The most intoxicating delusion of this mindset goes something like: "My code might not be as good as an [ICPC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Collegiate_Programming_Contest) god's, but I have deeper humanistic concerns. So even if these people are topping the leaderboards now, they'll end up working for me anyway." The most insidious toxicity of this class signaling is that it instrumentalizes humanism — dulling the rigor of the technical while hollowing out the depth of the humanistic. It is a double erosion of both, and, at heart, an intellectual self-flattery. Embracing mediocrity is, frankly, everyone's right. Choosing to coast through four years — or even a lifetime — or admitting that you simply view programming as a way to earn a living, is a form of professional honesty, and a form of honesty with yourself.

I have long felt that this class signaling mentality closely mirrors another one in academia: the blind disdain some researchers have for "paper milling" (salami slicing) and their pathological obsession with holding out for one grand hit. I once saw a biting critique from a professor at Texas A&M about exactly this mindset:

> Some people look down on prolific paper-millers, spending years thinking they're brewing a breakthrough on the level of [Kaiming He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiming_He) or [Fei-Fei Li](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fei-Fei_Li). Then, when they finally produce something, they realize it's not even as good as what big labs casually churn out on the side.

This captures the absurdity at the heart of academic vanity. Those who truly sharpen a single sword for a decade are the ones brave enough to tackle the hardest problems and slug it out in uncharted territory — not the ones hiding behind the banner of "long-termism" and wallowing in self-congratulation. The same logic applies to anyone who fancies himself as someone who "codes with one hand and reads Shakespeare with the other." When a person tries to dilute technical anxiety with the airs of the humanities, he ends up undermining the seriousness of both domains at once. In the end, he neither finds strength in the underlying logic of code, nor redemption in the tragedy of Shakespeare. This infatuation with using the liberal-arts pose as class signaling finally turns his life, through a double retreat in both logic and aesthetics, into an absurd tragedy of its own.

I have always believed that deciphering the ancient pronunciation of a word is just as important as discovering a minor planet. This is because dignity in any field is born only of the labor pains of solving complex problems. Rather than pretentious cultural name-dropping, I would much rather see someone weigh the tension in a word the way he wrings every drop of performance out of code — always with reverence for underlying logic, chasing that pure truth that refuses to be defined by disciplinary boundaries. After all, true elegance is never an excuse for evading complexity — it is the purity that emerges only after you have conquered it.

## Appendix: A Short Honesty Checklist

In a place like UWC, where expectations run sky-high, it's hard not to get swept up in some "perfect elite" narrative. If you have ever wondered whether you yourself might be doing a little Class Signaling, try sitting alone late one night and gently asking yourself a few questions:

**On the audience:**
If no one in your life ever knew what you were reading or studying — if no one gave you any credit for your "liberal-arts polish" — would the words or the logic still move you on their own? If that pure joy is gone, it may be because you have been carrying a script written for someone else's eyes.

**On superiority:**
Have you, somewhere beneath the surface, been using this interdisciplinary identity as a defense mechanism? When you hit a wall in one domain, do you soothe the setback by telling yourself "at least I have depth elsewhere"? Real depth is not a step stool for looking down on others; it is the ground beneath your feet when you walk through a valley.

**On sanctuary:**
Are you consuming the symbols of the humanities, or experiencing the spirit of the humanities? Admitting that you are tired and need to retreat into books for solace should be encouraged. What we need to distinguish is this: are you seeking a sanctuary that lets you keep living, or building a moat that advertises how different you are?

**On awkward honesty:**
Do you allow yourself to be "inelegant" when you cross disciplines? Genuine interdisciplinary exploration is almost always clumsy in its early stages. If you cannot bring yourself to admit that you are, in some field, really just a fumbling beginner — because it would damage your "bi-disciplinary" persona — then the persona itself has become the shackle on your growth.

**A final word:**

There is no shame in needing a "persona" from time to time; it can even serve as a social lubricant. But stay vigilant — do not let those signals become the wall between you and the real world, or between you and your real self. This piece is not meant as an indictment of anyone who opens a book between coding sessions. Quite the opposite: in an age where everyone wants to be the protagonist of some perfect-elite narrative, what I would rather pay tribute to is the courage of those who can admit they did not really understand Shakespeare, and the honesty of those who can calmly accept that their code is, in the end, nothing special. Because we never need to reach for another kind of altitude to cover up how messy we are right now.

The finest elegance is the one that arrives when you no longer need to prove anything to anyone, and yet still hold a quiet curiosity and reverence for how the world works — whether it is that stubborn line of code, or that obscure line of verse.
