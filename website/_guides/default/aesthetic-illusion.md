In the narratives of the "social elite," nothing is more elegant than the image of "coding with one hand while reading Shakespeare with the other." This polymath persona is incredibly seductive, as it perfectly taps into the yearning for class aesthetics and romantic narratives. But if you spend enough time in a major research lab, you'll realize that this narrative is often a highly dangerous form of aesthetic compensation.

In fact, I believe this "interdisciplinary elegance" is fundamentally isomorphic to another common obsession in academia: the so-called "ten-year sword" mentality — toiling in obscurity for one grand masterpiece. Both are cognitive sanctuaries constructed to evade the cutthroat reality of industrial-scale competition.

Why are so many people obsessed with Shakespeare? Because when someone feels their brain suffocating while wrestling with system deadlocks or optimizing CUDA kernels, retreating into the comfort zone of "humanistic depth" becomes the most dignified escape route.

At that moment, they tell themselves: "My code might not be as good as an [ICPC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Collegiate_Programming_Contest) god's, but I have deeper humanistic concerns. So even if these people are topping the leaderboards now, they'll end up working for me anyway." But in the era of [The Bitter Lesson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitter_lesson), this is a massive paradox. A system's loss function does not decrease because of your elegance. This "do-both" posture is, at its core, a form of hedging with mediocrity across multiple fronts, necessitated by an inability to break through in any single domain.

This sanctuary mentality is strikingly similar to the blind disdain some researchers have for "paper milling" (salami slicing) and their pathological obsession with "holding out for a magnum opus." I once saw a biting critique from a professor at Texas A&M regarding this mindset:

> Some people look down on prolific paper-millers, spending years thinking they're brewing a breakthrough on the level of [Kaiming He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiming_He) or [Fei-Fei Li](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fei-Fei_Li). Then, when they finally produce something, they realize it's not even as good as what big labs casually churn out on the side.

This captures the absurdity at the heart of academic vanity. Those who truly "sharpen a single sword for a decade" are the ones brave enough to tackle big problems and slug it out in uncharted territory — not the ones hiding behind the banner of "long-termism" and wallowing in self-congratulation. Similarly, for many of our peers, the cruelest truth only reveals itself at graduation: those who tried to use a Shakespearean veneer to hedge against the anxiety of being unable to write high-performance code eventually discover they can neither write production-grade code nor possess an understanding of the world and the industry as profound as those who were forged in the "trenches" of labs and competitions. This is because profound insight is never read—it is grown from the labor pains of solving complex problems; it is realized through intellectual collisions with giants in the field. This is far more powerful than any pretentious cultural name-dropping.

By the time you've read this far, you might feel I am being overly dismissive of the humanities — but that is not to say I dismiss their value. I have always believed that deciphering the ancient pronunciation of a word is just as important as discovering a minor planet. This is because dignity in any field is born only of the labor pains of solving complex problems. I do not reject Shakespeare. I only hope that, when you weigh the tension within a word, you do it the way you wring every drop of performance out of code — always with reverence for underlying logic, chasing that pure truth that refuses to be defined by disciplinary boundaries. After all, true elegance is never an excuse for evading complexity — it is the purity that emerges only after you have conquered it.
