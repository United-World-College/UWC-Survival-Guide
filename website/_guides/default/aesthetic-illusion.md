In the so-called "social elite" narratives circulating on social media, nothing is more elegant than the image of coding with one hand and reading Shakespeare with the other. Picture it: late at night in a café, CUDA kernels pulsing across the screen on the left, an open copy of *Hamlet* resting just so on the right. It happens to be exactly the recipe that certain elite-education pipelines hold most dear. This "polymath" persona is incredibly seductive, as it perfectly taps into the yearning for class aesthetics and romantic narratives. But if you spend enough time in a major research lab, you'll realize that this narrative is often a highly dangerous form of aesthetic compensation.

Why are so many people obsessed with Shakespeare? Because when someone feels their brain suffocating while wrestling with system deadlocks or optimizing CUDA kernels, treating humanistic depth as a comfort zone becomes the most dignified escape. But this is itself an illusion — the real Shakespeare is anything but gentle. He lays bare the bloody workings of power and the darkest corners of human nature, and unpacking him is no easier than unpacking any complex distributed system. What people call "retreating into the humanities" is really just retreating into a fantasy of the humanities. The same goes the other way: the "code machines" who stack logic relentlessly yet refuse to reflect on the meaning of their work are practicing another form of the very same escape.

The most intoxicating delusion of this mindset goes something like: "My code might not be as good as an [ICPC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Collegiate_Programming_Contest) god's, but I have deeper humanistic concerns. So even if these people are topping the leaderboards now, they'll end up working for me anyway." The most insidious toxicity of this narrative is that it instrumentalizes humanism — dulling the rigor of the technical while hollowing out the depth of the humanistic. It is a double erosion of both, and, at heart, an intellectual self-flattery. Embracing mediocrity is, frankly, everyone's right. Choosing to coast through four years — or even a lifetime — or admitting that you simply view programming as a way to earn a living, is a form of professional honesty, and a form of honesty with yourself.

I have long felt that this sanctuary mentality closely mirrors another one in academia: the blind disdain some researchers have for "paper milling" (salami slicing) and their pathological obsession with holding out for one grand hit. I once saw a biting critique from a professor at Texas A&M about exactly this mindset:

> Some people look down on prolific paper-millers, spending years thinking they're brewing a breakthrough on the level of [Kaiming He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiming_He) or [Fei-Fei Li](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fei-Fei_Li). Then, when they finally produce something, they realize it's not even as good as what big labs casually churn out on the side.

This captures the absurdity at the heart of academic vanity. Those who truly sharpen a single sword for a decade are the ones brave enough to tackle the hardest problems and slug it out in uncharted territory — not the ones hiding behind the banner of "long-termism" and wallowing in self-congratulation. The same logic applies to anyone who fancies himself as someone who "codes with one hand and reads Shakespeare with the other." When a person tries to dilute technical anxiety with the airs of the humanities, he ends up undermining the seriousness of both domains at once. In the end, he neither finds strength in the underlying logic of code, nor redemption in the tragedy of Shakespeare. This infatuation with an aura of interdisciplinary superiority finally turns his life, through a double retreat in both logic and aesthetics, into an absurd tragedy of its own.

I have always believed that deciphering the ancient pronunciation of a word is just as important as discovering a minor planet. This is because dignity in any field is born only of the labor pains of solving complex problems. Rather than pretentious cultural name-dropping, I would much rather see someone weigh the tension in a word the way he wrings every drop of performance out of code — always with reverence for underlying logic, chasing that pure truth that refuses to be defined by disciplinary boundaries. After all, true elegance is never an excuse for evading complexity — it is the purity that emerges only after you have conquered it.
